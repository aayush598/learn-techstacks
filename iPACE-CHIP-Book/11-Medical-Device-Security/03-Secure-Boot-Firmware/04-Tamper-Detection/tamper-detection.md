# Tamper Detection for iPACE-CHIP

## Overview

Tamper detection is the capability of the iPACE-CHIP implantable pacemaker to
identify physical or logical intrusion attempts and respond appropriately to
protect sensitive data and maintain patient safety. As a life-critical implant,
the iPACE-CHIP must detect and respond to tampering that could compromise its
therapy delivery, expose patient data, or enable unauthorized modification of
its operation. This chapter covers the multi-layered tamper detection architecture,
response mechanisms, and the integration of tamper evidence into the device's
security posture.

## 1. Tamper Threat Model

### 1.1 Physical Tamper Scenarios

| Attack | Objective | Sophistication | Detection Method |
|--------|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Decapsulation | Access die surface | High (lab equipment) | Active mesh, light sensor |
| Microprobing | Read/write signals | Very high | Active mesh, EM sensor |
| Laser fault injection | Flip bits | High | Voltage monitoring, redundancy |
| Voltage glitching | Bypass checks | Medium | Voltage supervisor |
| Clock manipulation | Timing attacks | Medium | Clock monitor |
| Thermal analysis | Extract keys | High | Thermal sensor |
| EM emanation analysis | Side-channel | High | Shielding, noise generation |
| Physical relocation | Remove device | Low | Movement sensor, integrity check |

### 1.2 Logical Tamper Scenarios

| Attack | Objective | Detection Method |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|
| Debug port access | Extract keys | Debug lock fuse, intrusion detect |
| Memory bus snooping | Read key data | Bus encryption, access control |
| Firmware modification | Alter behavior | Integrity checks, signature verify |
| Parameter manipulation | Change therapy | Bounds checking, dual verification |
| Replay attacks | Inject old data | Sequence counters, nonce verification |

### 1.3 Adversary Capability Classes

Class 1 (Opportunistic): Consumer tools, external access only, high detection confidence.

Class 2 (Skilled): Oscilloscope, logic analyzer, limited internal access via X-ray,
high detection confidence.

Class 3 (Advanced): FIB, SEM, laser probe station, full die-level access, medium
detection confidence through active measures.

Class 4 (Nation-state): Custom equipment, unlimited budget, full physical access
over extended time, defense-in-depth with multiple layers.

## 2. Physical Tamper Detection

### 2.1 Active Shield Mesh

The iPACE-CHIP implements an active conductive mesh over the die surface that
detects physical intrusion attempts:

Mesh Specifications:
- Material: Aluminum alloy, 0.5 micron width
- Layer: Top metal layer,覆盖 entire die
- Resistance: 100 ohms nominal per segment
- Detection threshold: Plus or minus 20 percent deviation
- Response time: Less than 100 nanoseconds
- Coverage: 98.5 percent of die area

Operating Principle:
The mesh carries a continuous AC signal generated by an on-chip oscillator.
Any physical intrusion that cuts or deforms the mesh changes its impedance,
which is detected by a comparator circuit. The AC signal frequency (10 MHz)
prevents DC-based bypass attacks.

Mesh Segmentation:
The mesh is divided into 16 independent segments, each monitored separately.
This enables detection of localized intrusion and prevents a single bypass
from defeating the entire mesh.

Tamper Detection Circuit:
A lock-and-reset circuit monitors mesh impedance continuously. When deviation
exceeds the threshold for more than 1 microsecond (debounce), the tamper flag
is asserted and the zeroization sequence begins.

### 2.2 Light Sensor Array

The iPACE-CHIP includes photodiodes to detect laser and light-based attacks:

- 8 photodiodes distributed across the die surface
- Sensitivity: 400 to 1100 nanometers wavelength
- Response time: Less than 10 nanoseconds
- Detection threshold: 1 microwatt per square centimeter
- Coverage: Critical areas (KMU, crypto engine, OTP)

During decapsulation, the removal of the opaque packaging exposes the die
to ambient light, triggering the photodiode array. Even focused laser beams
used for fault injection are detected by the nearest photodiode.

### 2.3 Electromagnetic Sensor

An on-chip EM sensor detects electromagnetic emanation from external probes:

- Pickup coil: On-chip spiral inductor, 10 turns
- Sensitivity: 1 millivolt per meter
- Frequency range: 1 MHz to 1 GHz
- Response time: Less than 50 nanoseconds
- Detection threshold: 3x normal emanation level

The EM sensor detects the electromagnetic fields generated by microprobing
equipment and near-field EM probes used for side-channel analysis.

### 2.4 Temperature Sensor

On-chip temperature monitoring detects thermal analysis attacks:

- Sensor type: Bandgap reference temperature sensor
- Resolution: 0.5 degrees Celsius
- Range: -40 to +85 degrees Celsius (operating), alerts outside range
- Response time: 1 millisecond
- Tamper threshold: Rapid temperature change greater than 5 degrees per second

Thermal analysis attacks often involve localized heating or cooling of the die.
Rapid temperature changes outside normal operating parameters trigger the
tamper response.

## 3. Electrical Tamper Detection

### 3.1 Voltage Supervisor

The voltage supervisor monitors all power rails for glitching attacks:

Monitored Rails:
- VDD_CORE: 1.0V nominal, plus or minus 5 percent
- VDD_IO: 1.8V nominal, plus or minus 5 percent
- VDD_ANA: 1.8V nominal, plus or minus 3 percent
- VBAT: 2.0 to 3.6V (battery voltage)

Detection Capabilities:
- Undervoltage: less than 95 percent of nominal
- Overvoltage: greater than 105 percent of nominal
- Glitch detection: voltage transient greater than 10 percent in less than 10 nanoseconds
- Brownout: sustained undervoltage greater than 1 millisecond
- Power cycling: more than 3 power cycles in 1 second

Response:
- Voltage glitch: immediate tamper response
- Undervoltage: graceful shutdown, save state
- Overvoltage: immediate power disconnect (hardware)
- Power cycling: enter lockout mode

### 3.2 Clock Monitor

The clock monitor detects clock manipulation attacks:

Monitored Clocks:
- Main oscillator: 24 MHz (nominal, plus or minus 100 ppm)
- Low-frequency: 32.768 kHz (nominal, plus or minus 50 ppm)
- TRNG clock: Ring oscillator (self-correcting)

Detection:
- Frequency deviation greater than 1 percent
- Clock missing for more than 10 microseconds
- Clock glitch: missing pulse greater than 1 clock cycle
- Metastability: dual-edge detection on critical flip-flops

Response:
- Clock anomaly detected: switch to internal backup oscillator
- If backup also anomalous: halt and zeroize keys

### 3.3 Current Monitor

The iPACE-CHIP monitors power consumption for anomalous patterns:

- Normal operation current: 50 to 200 microamps
- Crypto operation current: 500 to 2000 microamps
- Tamper threshold: greater than 5000 microamps (possible short circuit or probing)
- Sub-threshold monitoring: current less than 10 microamps (possible open circuit)

Anomalous current patterns can indicate:
- External power injection (active probing)
- Short circuits from microprobing needles
- EM probe coupling to power rails

## 4. Logical Tamper Detection

### 4.1 Debug Port Protection

The iPACE-CHIP debug interface is protected against unauthorized access:

JTAG/SWD Configuration:
- Production mode: debug port permanently disabled via one-time fuse
- Recovery: impossible by design (prevents post-deployment debug access)
- Fuse verification: checked at every boot
- Tamper on fuse bypass attempt: immediate key zeroization

Debug Port Monitoring:
- Physical tamper detect: light sensor around debug pads
- Voltage monitoring: debug pad voltage thresholds
- Protocol monitoring: invalid JTAG/SWD sequences logged
- Timing monitoring: access attempts outside normal patterns

### 4.2 Memory Protection Unit

The ARM Cortex-M4 Memory Protection Unit (MPU) enforces access control:

Protection Regions:
- Boot ROM: read-only, no execute from data bus
- SBL/ABL: read-only after verification
- KMU registers: write-only from CPU (keys not readable)
- Application flash: read-execute, no direct write
- SRAM: read-write, no execute (prevent code injection)
- Peripheral registers: access control per security level

MPU Violation Response:
- Any unauthorized access: hard fault exception
- Hard fault handler: log violation, zeroize keys, enter safe mode
- Multiple violations: immediate device halt

### 4.3 Stack Canaries and Buffer Overflow Protection

```
Stack Protection:
  - Stack canary: random value placed before return address
  - Canary verification: checked before every function return
  - Canary source: TRNG-generated, refreshed at boot
  - On canary corruption: immediate reset and tamper log

Buffer Overflow Detection:
  - MPU-enforced stack boundaries
  - Heap overflow detection: guard pages
  - Return address integrity: optional ARM PAC (Pointer Authentication)
```

### 4.4 Runtime Integrity Monitoring

Continuous monitoring of critical firmware regions:

Monitored Regions:
- Interrupt vector table: SHA-256 every 60 seconds
- Critical handler code: SHA-256 every 300 seconds
- Crypto functions: SHA-256 every 60 seconds
- Safety monitor code: SHA-256 every 60 seconds

Verification Method:
- Background hash computation during idle cycles
- Comparison with expected hash (stored in OTP for boot ROM,
  derived from signature for application code)
- On mismatch: immediate tamper response

## 5. Tamper Response

### 5.1 Response Levels

| Level | Trigger | Response | Recovery |
|-------|---------|----------|----------|
| Level 1 (Alert) | Anomalous temperature, minor voltage deviation | Log event, continue operation | Automatic |
| Level 2 (Warning) | Mesh deviation 10-20%, EM detection | Log event, increase monitoring | Automatic |
| Level 3 (Tamper) | Mesh breach, light detection, debug attempt | Zeroize all volatile keys, log event | Re-pair required |
| Level 4 (Critical) | Multiple simultaneous detections, key extraction attempt | Zeroize ALL keys (including NVM), halt | Factory reset required |

### 5.2 Key Zeroization Protocol

Upon tamper detection, the following zeroization sequence executes:

Zeroization Steps:
1. Assert tamper signal (hardware, less than 10 nanoseconds)
2. KMU immediately zeros all volatile key registers (triple overwrite)
3. NVM key slots: HMAC invalidated, key data overwritten
4. Session state: all nonces and counters zeroized
5. OTP keys: cannot be zeroized (immutable), but derived keys destroyed
6. Zeroization complete flag set
7. Tamper event logged in tamper-resistant log

Zeroization Timing:
- Volatile keys: 167 nanoseconds (triple overwrite + verify)
- NVM keys: 10 milliseconds (3-pass overwrite)
- Full zeroization: 10.2 milliseconds

### 5.3 Safe Mode Operation

After a Level 3 or Level 4 tamper response:

Safe Mode Behavior:
1. Device enters minimal operational mode
2. Maintain current therapy parameters (cannot be changed)
3. All wireless communication disabled
4. NFC programming mode available (requires clinician authentication)
5. Tamper event details available for forensic download
6. Device remains in safe mode until clinician intervention

Safe Mode Limitations:
- No parameter changes without full re-authentication
- No firmware updates without emergency procedure
- Limited telemetry (basic status only)
- Patient controller connection refused

### 5.4 Tamper Evident Logging

All tamper events are recorded in a tamper-evident log:

Log Entry Format:
- Timestamp: UTC (8 bytes)
- Event type: enumeration (2 bytes)
- Detection source: bitmask (2 bytes)
- Severity: Level 1-4 (1 byte)
- Context: 16 bytes (sensor readings at time of detection)
- Previous entry hash: SHA-256 (32 bytes)
- Entry hash: SHA-256 of all preceding fields (32 bytes)

Log Properties:
- Append-only (cannot be modified without breaking hash chain)
- Stored in protected NVM region
- 100 entries maximum (oldest evicted FIFO)
- Downloadable via NFC for forensic analysis
- Signed by device attestation key

## 6. Tamper Detection Circuitry

### 6.1 Hardware Implementation

Tamper Detection Block:
- Separate power domain (always-on, battery-backed)
- Independent clock source (32.768 kHz)
- Dedicated SRAM for tamper state
- Hardware state machine (no firmware dependency)
- Direct connection to KMU for zeroization

Power Consumption:
- Active monitoring: 0.5 microamps
- Tamper response: 2 milliamps (for 10 milliseconds)
- Always-on requirement: compatible with implant battery life

### 6.2 Sensor Integration

All tamper sensors feed into a central tamper controller:

Sensor Inputs:
- Active mesh: 16 channels
- Photodiodes: 8 channels
- EM sensor: 1 channel (analog)
- Temperature: 2 channels
- Voltage: 4 channels
- Clock: 3 channels
- Current: 1 channel
- Debug port: 2 channels

Total: 37 sensor inputs

Tamper Controller Logic:
- Combines multiple sensor inputs
- Applies debounce (1 microsecond for transient)
- Implements voting logic (2-of-3 for Level 3)
- Maintains tamper state history
- Generates tamper interrupt and zeroization signal

### 6.3 Anti-Bypass Measures

To prevent attackers from bypassing tamper detection:

Countermeasure 1: Mesh Redundancy
- Two independent mesh layers (top and bottom metal)
- Different frequencies (10 MHz and 15 MHz)
- Both must be intact for normal operation

Countermeasure 2: Sensor Diversity
- Multiple sensor types detect same attack class
- No single bypass defeats all sensors
- Voting logic requires agreement of 2+ sensors

Countermeasure 3: Active Challenge-Response
- Tamper controller sends random challenges to sensors
- Sensors must respond correctly within 1 microsecond
- Prevents sensor spoofing with fixed voltages

Countermeasure 4: Continuous Self-Test
- Sensors tested against known-good values every 60 seconds
- Any sensor failure logged as potential tamper
- Degraded sensor operation triggers enhanced monitoring

## 7. Implantation Integrity

### 7.1 Post-Implantation Verification

After surgical implantation, the iPACE-CHIP performs integrity verification:

Verification Steps:
1. Power-on self-test (POST)
2. Tamper mesh integrity check
3. Sensor calibration verification
4. Key storage integrity (HMAC check on NVM keys)
5. Firmware integrity (Merkle tree verification)
6. Communication test (NFC response to programmer)
7. Therapy parameter verification

Timing: Complete within 5 seconds of power-on
Patient Impact: No disruption to ongoing therapy

### 7.2 Movement Detection

The iPACE-CHIP includes an accelerometer for motion detection:

Specifications:
- 3-axis MEMS accelerometer
- Range: plus or minus 2g
- Resolution: 12-bit (0.5 mg per LSB)
- Sample rate: 100 Hz
- Power consumption: 10 microamps

Motion-Based Security:
- Unusual motion patterns may indicate device removal
- Motion threshold for tamper: greater than 10g (impact/shock)
- Sustained motion: device relocation detected
- Correlation with other sensors: multi-factor tamper assessment

### 7.3 Encapsulant Integrity

The medical-grade encapsulant protecting the die is monitored:

- Capacitive sensing: encapsulant dielectric constant changes with damage
- Moisture ingress detection: humidity sensor near package seal
- Package crack detection: acoustic emission sensor (piezoelectric)
- Hermetic seal verification: internal pressure monitoring

## 10. Summary

The iPACE-CHIP implements a comprehensive multi-layered tamper detection system
combining physical sensors (active mesh, photodiodes, EM sensors), electrical
monitors (voltage, clock, current), and logical protections (MPU, debug lock,
integrity monitoring). The system detects attacks at all sophistication levels,
from opportunistic to nation-state. Tamper response escalates from logging
through key zeroization to complete device lockdown. All tamper events are
recorded in tamper-evident logs with hash-chain integrity for forensic analysis.
The hardware-based tamper controller operates independently of the main processor,
ensuring protection even if firmware is compromised. The design meets or exceeds
the tamper resistance requirements of FIPS 140-3 Level 2, IEC 62443-4-2 SL3,
and FDA cybersecurity guidance for implantable medical devices.
