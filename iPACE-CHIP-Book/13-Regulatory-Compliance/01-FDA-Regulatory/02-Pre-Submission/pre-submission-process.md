# Pre-Submission (Q-Sub) Process for iPACE-CHIP

## 1. Introduction to Pre-Submission Program

The FDA Pre-Submission program, also known as the Q-Sub (Question and Submission) program, provides a structured mechanism for medical device manufacturers to obtain FDA feedback before making a formal regulatory submission. For the iPACE-CHIP implantable pacemaker, the Pre-Submission program is an invaluable tool for reducing regulatory risk and streamlining the 510(k) clearance process.

### 1.1 Purpose of Pre-Submission

The Pre-Submission program serves several key purposes:

- Clarify regulatory expectations and requirements
- Identify appropriate predicate device(s)
- Define the scope of performance testing required
- Resolve questions about biocompatibility testing
- Address software documentation requirements
- Discuss clinical data needs
- Review labeling requirements

### 1.2 Types of Pre-Submission Meetings

The FDA offers several types of pre-submission interactions:

| Meeting Type | Format | Response Timeline |
|-------------|--------|-------------------|
| Type A (Dispute Resolution) | In-person/Virtual | 30 calendar days |
| Type B (Premarket) | In-person/Virtual | 75 calendar days |
| Type C (General) | In-person/Virtual | 90 calendar days |
| Type D (Early Feedback) | Written | 90 calendar days |
| Pre-Submission (Q-Sub) | Written | 90 calendar days |

### 1.3 Benefits for iPACE-CHIP

Engaging in the Pre-Submission process provides:

- Early identification of potential regulatory obstacles
- Clarification of testing requirements
- Feedback on predicate device selection
- Guidance on clinical evidence needs
- Reduced risk of 510(k) rejection or refusal
- Shorter overall time to market

## 2. Preparing a Pre-Submission Request

### 2.1 Request for Pre-Submission

The Pre-Submission request is submitted via the FDA's CDRH portal and must include:

#### Cover Letter
- Clear statement requesting a Pre-Submission meeting
- Brief description of the device and intended use
- Specific questions for FDA feedback
- Preferred meeting format (in-person, virtual, or teleconference)

#### Device Description
- Comprehensive device description
- Intended use statement
- Target patient population
- Comparison to predicate device(s)
- Summary of technological characteristics

#### Specific Questions for FDA
The questions should be specific, clear, and actionable. Examples for iPACE-CHIP:

1. What predicate device does FDA consider most appropriate for the iPACE-CHIP 510(k) submission?
2. Does the proposed biocompatibility testing plan adequately address all material-contact combinations?
3. Is the proposed software documentation package sufficient for the identified Level of Concern?
4. Does the proposed clinical study design provide adequate clinical evidence?
5. Is the proposed EMC testing plan adequate for an implantable cardiac pacemaker?

### 2.2 Draft Pre-Submission Package

A well-prepared Pre-Submission package includes:

- Executive summary (2–3 pages)
- Device description with diagrams
- Predicate comparison table
- Specific questions with supporting rationale
- Proposed testing plan
- Draft labeling (if available)
- Supporting literature (if applicable)

### 2.3 Pre-Submission Quality Considerations

The quality of the Pre-Submission package directly impacts the quality of FDA feedback:

- **Clarity:** Questions must be unambiguous
- **Specificity:** Avoid vague or open-ended questions
- **Context:** Provide sufficient background information
- **Actionability:** FDA can only provide feedback on actionable questions
- **Relevance:** Questions must relate to the specific device and submission strategy

## 3. Pre-Submission Meeting Process

### 3.1 Submission and Scheduling

The typical timeline for a Pre-Submission meeting includes:

- **Day 0:** Submit Pre-Submission request via CDRH portal
- **Day 14–21:** FDA acknowledges receipt and assigns reviewer
- **Day 30–45:** FDA distributes draft agenda and briefing materials
- **Day 45–60:** Meeting scheduled (typically 4–6 weeks from acknowledgment)
- **Day 60–75:** Meeting conducted
- **Day 75–90:** FDA provides written meeting minutes

### 3.2 Meeting Preparation

#### Pre-Meeting Activities
- Review FDA's draft agenda and briefing materials
- Prepare responses to FDA's questions
- Identify subject matter experts to attend
- Prepare presentation materials
- Rehearse meeting logistics (AV, remote access)

#### Meeting Agenda
A typical Pre-Submission meeting agenda includes:

1. **Introduction and Opening Remarks** (5 minutes)
   - Attendee introductions
   - Meeting objectives and ground rules

2. **Device Overview Presentation** (15–20 minutes)
   - Intended use and indications
   - Device description and design
   - Predicate comparison
   - Proposed regulatory pathway

3. **Technical Discussion** (30–45 minutes)
   - Performance testing requirements
   - Biocompatibility considerations
   - Software documentation needs
   - Clinical evidence requirements

4. **Open Discussion and Questions** (15–20 minutes)
   - Address any remaining questions
   - Clarify action items and next steps

5. **Closing Remarks** (5 minutes)
   - Summary of key decisions
   - Timeline for follow-up actions

### 3.3 During the Meeting

Key practices during the meeting:

- **Active listening:** Understand FDA's perspective before responding
- **Note-taking:** Document all key points and action items
- **Technical depth:** Be prepared to discuss technical details
- **Honesty:** Acknowledge limitations and uncertainties
- **Collaboration:** Approach discussions as a collaborative process

### 3.4 Meeting Documentation

Meeting minutes must be documented and include:

- Attendees (FDA and manufacturer)
- Key discussion points
- FDA feedback and recommendations
- Action items with responsible parties and timelines
- Agreed-upon testing requirements
- Any changes to the proposed submission strategy

## 4. FDA Feedback and Response

### 4.1 Interpreting FDA Feedback

FDA feedback may include:

- **Agreement:** FDA agrees with the proposed approach
- **Partial agreement:** FDA agrees with modifications
- **Disagreement:** FDA recommends alternative approach
- **Information request:** FDA needs additional information
- **No comment:** FDA declines to comment on specific questions

### 4.2 Responding to FDA Feedback

The manufacturer should:

- Review all feedback with the regulatory team
- Assess impact on the submission strategy
- Develop a response plan addressing each item
- Implement necessary changes to testing or documentation
- Document all changes and rationale

### 4.3 Follow-Up Submissions

If significant changes are made based on FDA feedback, a follow-up Pre-Submission may be warranted:

- Major changes to the predicate strategy
- New testing requirements identified
- Clinical data requirements changed
- Software documentation scope expanded

- Multiple material-contact combinations
- Long-term implantation (>30 days)
- Blood-contacting surfaces
- Lead body and insulation materials
- Generator housing materials

#### Pre-Submission Topics for Biocompatibility
- Material characterization completeness
- Testing matrix adequacy
- Justification for any testing omissions
- Special considerations for novel materials

### 5.3 Software Documentation

The software documentation requirements for implantable pacemakers are extensive:

- Software Level of Concern determination
- IEC 62304 compliance plan
- Cybersecurity risk assessment
- Artificial intelligence/machine learning considerations (if applicable)

### 5.4 Clinical Evidence

For implantable pacemakers, clinical evidence may include:

- Literature review and meta-analysis
- Clinical study protocol (if needed)
- Real-world evidence plans
- Post-market study commitments

### 5.5 Electromagnetic Compatibility

EMC considerations for implantable pacemakers:

- MRI conditional labeling (if applicable)

## 6. Pre-Submission Case Studies

### 6.1 Case Study 1: Novel Pacing Algorithm

**Background:** iPACE-CHIP includes a novel adaptive rate-responsive pacing algorithm that uses MEMS-based accelerometer data.

**Pre-Submission Question:** Does FDA agree that the proposed accelerated bench testing protocol adequately demonstrates the safety and effectiveness of the adaptive rate algorithm?

**FDA Response:** FDA agreed that the proposed testing was adequate but recommended additional testing under extreme temperature conditions.

**Outcome:** Additional thermal testing was added to the protocol, saving significant time during 510(k) review.

### 6.2 Case Study 2: Leadless Design Considerations

**Background:** iPACE-CHIP incorporates a leadless design with direct myocardial fixation.

**Pre-Submission Question:** What additional biocompatibility testing is required for the novel fixation mechanism?

**FDA Response:** FDA recommended additional implantation testing with histopathological evaluation of the tissue-device interface.

**Outcome:** Expanded implantation study was designed, ensuring adequate data for the submission.

### 6.3 Case Study 3: Cybersecurity Architecture

**Background:** iPACE-CHIP includes wireless Bluetooth Low Energy communication for external programming.

**Pre-Submission Question:** Does the proposed cybersecurity architecture meet FDA's premarket cybersecurity guidance requirements?

**FDA Response:** FDA requested additional penetration testing and a more detailed threat model.

**Outcome:** Enhanced cybersecurity documentation was developed, resulting in a stronger submission.

## 8. Common Pre-Submission Questions for Implantable Pacemakers

### 8.1 General Questions

1. Does FDA agree with the proposed device classification and regulatory pathway?
2. Is the proposed predicate device appropriate for demonstrating substantial equivalence?
3. Are there any known special controls or general controls that apply to this device?

### 8.2 Performance Testing Questions

4. Does FDA agree with the proposed performance testing protocol?
5. Are there any additional performance tests required for this device type?
6. Does the proposed sample size provide adequate statistical power?

### 8.3 Biocompatibility Questions

7. Does the proposed biocompatibility testing plan adequately address all material-contact combinations?
8. Are there any special biocompatibility considerations for this device type?
9. Does the biological risk assessment justify any testing omissions?

### 8.4 Software Questions

10. Does FDA agree with the proposed software Level of Concern classification?
11. Is the proposed software documentation package sufficient?
12. Are there any specific cybersecurity requirements beyond the general guidance?

### 8.5 Clinical Questions

13. Does FDA agree that clinical data from literature is sufficient, or is a clinical study required?
14. If a clinical study is required, does FDA agree with the proposed study design?
15. What are the minimum follow-up duration and endpoints?

## 9. Pre-Submission Success Factors

### 9.1 Preparation Excellence

- **Thorough research:** Review FDA guidance documents and precedent decisions
- **Quality documentation:** Prepare clear, concise, and well-organized materials
- **Expert team:** Include subject matter experts from all relevant disciplines
- **Mock meetings:** Practice the meeting with internal stakeholders

### 9.2 Question Formulation

- **Specificity:** Each question should be narrow and focused
- **Actionability:** FDA should be able to provide a definitive response
- **Context:** Provide enough background for FDA to understand the question
- **Prioritization:** Rank questions by importance

### 9.3 Meeting Management

- **Time management:** Stay on agenda and respect time limits
- **Technical depth:** Be prepared for detailed technical discussions
- **Flexibility:** Adapt to unexpected discussion points
- **Documentation:** Capture all feedback and action items

### 9.4 Post-Meeting Follow-Up

- **Timely response:** Address FDA feedback promptly
- **Thorough documentation:** Document all changes made in response
- **Communication:** Maintain open communication with the FDA review team
- **Quality implementation:** Ensure all agreed-upon changes are properly implemented

## 10. Pre-Submission vs. Formal Submission

### 10.1 Comparison of Approaches

| Aspect | Pre-Submission | Formal 510(k) |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| Purpose | Get FDA feedback | Get FDA clearance |
| Binding | Non-binding advice | Binding decision |
| Timeline | 90 days | 90 days (+ extensions) |
| Format | Flexible | Rigid (RTA criteria) |
| Cost | Time and resources | User fee required |
| Risk | Low | High |

### 10.2 When to Use Pre-Submission

The Pre-Submission program is recommended when:

- First-time 510(k) submission for a novel device
- Uncertain about predicate device selection
- Complex biocompatibility or clinical questions
- Novel software or cybersecurity architecture
- Significant changes to a previously cleared device

### 10.3 When to Skip Pre-Submission

A Pre-Submission may not be necessary when:

- The device is substantially similar to a previous submission
- Testing requirements are well-established
- The predicate device is clearly appropriate
- The manufacturer has extensive 510(k) experience
