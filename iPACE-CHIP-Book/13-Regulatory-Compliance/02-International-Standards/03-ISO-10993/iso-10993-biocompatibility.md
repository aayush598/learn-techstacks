# ISO 10993: Biocompatibility Evaluation for iPACE-CHIP

## 1. Introduction to ISO 10993

ISO 10993 is the international standard series for the biological evaluation of medical devices. For the iPACE-CHIP implantable pacemaker, ISO 10993 compliance is fundamental to demonstrating that all patient-contacting materials are safe for long-term implantation. This chapter provides a comprehensive guide to the ISO 10993 standard series and its application to the iPACE-CHIP.

### 1.1 Standard Series Overview

ISO 10993 consists of multiple parts addressing different aspects of biocompatibility evaluation:

| Part | Title | Applicability to iPACE-CHIP |
|------|-------|---------------------------|
| ISO 10993-1 | Evaluation and testing within a risk management process | Framework standard |

### 1.2 Regulatory Significance

ISO 10993 compliance is recognized by all major regulatory authorities:

- FDA: Referenced in 21 CFR 870.3440 special controls and FDA Blue Book Memorandum #G95-1
- EU MDR: Harmonized standard under EU MDR 2017/745
- Health Canada: Recognized consensus standard
- TGA: Essential principle compliance
- CDSCO: Reference standard for Indian market

### 1.3 Relationship to Other Standards

ISO 10993 works in concert with:

- **ISO 14971:** Risk management integration
- **IEC 60601-1:** General safety requirements
- **ISO 14708:** Active implantable medical device requirements
- **ASTM F748:** Standard practice for selecting generic biological test methods
- **USP <87>, <88>:** United States Pharmacopeia biological reactivity tests

## 2. Biological Evaluation Framework

### 2.1 ISO 10993-1: Evaluation and Testing Framework

#### 2.1.1 Purpose
ISO 10993-1 establishes the framework for biological evaluation of medical devices, including:

- Risk-based approach to testing
- Evaluation of existing data
- Identification of testing requirements
- Biological evaluation report preparation

#### 2.1.2 Risk-Based Approach
The biological evaluation must be based on a risk assessment that considers:

- Material characterization
- Nature and degree of body contact
- Contact duration classification
- Existing biocompatibility data
- Device-specific considerations

### 2.2 Contact Classification for iPACE-CHIP

#### 2.2.1 Nature of Contact
| Component | Contact Type | Description |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Generator housing | Tissue/bone contact | Long-term implantation |
| Lead body | Blood contact | Long-term implantation |
| Lead tip | Blood/tissue contact | Long-term implantation |
| Connector | Tissue contact | Long-term implantation |

#### 2.2.2 Duration of Contact
| Duration Category | Duration | iPACE-CHIP Applicability |
|-------------------|----------|--------------------------|
| Limited | ≤24 hours | Not applicable |
| Prolonged | 24 hours–30 days | Not applicable |
| Permanent | >30 days | **Applicable** - Long-term implantation |

### 2.3 Material Characterization

#### 2.3.1 Required Characterization
All patient-contacting materials must be fully characterized:

- Chemical composition and formulation
- Manufacturing process specifications
- Material certification and traceability
- Known additives, catalysts, and processing aids
- Surface treatment and finish
- Sterilization compatibility

#### 2.3.2 Material Categories

| Material | Application in iPACE-CHIP | Contact Type |
|----------|--------------------------|--------------|
| Titanium alloy | Generator housing | Tissue contact |
| Silicone rubber | Lead insulation | Blood/tissue contact |
| Polyurethane | Lead insulation | Blood/tissue contact |
| Platinum-iridium | Lead conductor/tip | Blood/tissue contact |
| Epoxy resin | Connector header | Tissue contact |
| Silicone gel | Battery encapsulant | Tissue contact |

### 2.4 Testing Matrix Development

The testing matrix is developed based on:

1. Material characterization results
2. Contact type and duration
3. Existing biocompatibility data
4. Risk assessment outcomes
5. Regulatory requirements

## 3. Cytotoxicity Testing (ISO 10993-5)

### 3.1 Purpose and Scope

Cytotoxicity testing evaluates the potential of device materials to cause cytotoxic effects through direct contact or extract exposure. This is typically the first biological test performed.

### 3.2 Test Methods

#### 3.2.1 Direct Contact Test
- Cell monolayer exposed directly to device material
- Assessment of cell morphology and viability
- Quantitative and qualitative endpoints

#### 3.2.2 Extract Test
- Device material extracted in culture medium
- Cell culture exposed to extraction fluid
- Cell viability measurement (MTT assay, neutral red uptake)

#### 3.2.3 Agar Diffusion Test
- Cell monolayer covered with agar
- Device material placed on agar surface
- Zone of inhibition measurement

### 3.3 Sample Requirements

| Parameter | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Sample size | 3 extracts minimum |
| Extraction conditions | 37°C for 72 hours |
| Extraction vehicle | Culture medium or saline |
| Positive control | Known cytotoxic material |
| Negative control | Known non-cytotoxic material |

### 3.4 Acceptance Criteria

- Negative control: Grade 0–1 reactivity
- Positive control: Grade 2–4 reactivity
- Test samples: Grade 0–1 reactivity acceptable
- Grade 2: Borderline, may require additional testing
- Grade 3–4: Unacceptable, material reformulation required

## 4. Sensitization Testing (ISO 10993-10)

### 4.1 Purpose and Scope

Sensitization testing evaluates the potential of device materials to cause delayed-type hypersensitivity reactions (allergic contact dermatitis).

### 4.2 Test Methods

#### 4.2.1 Guinea Pig Maximization Test (GPMT)
- Intradermal induction with adjuvant
- Topical induction
- Challenge exposure
- Sensitivity grading

#### 4.2.2 Local Lymph Node Assay (LLNA)
- Mouse ear model
- Lymph node proliferation measurement
- Stimulation index calculation
- Concentration-response analysis

### 4.3 Sample Requirements

| Parameter | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| GPMT | 10 test animals + 5 control animals |
| LLNA | 4 animals per group, 3–4 dose groups |
| Positive control | Known sensitizer (e.g., DNCB) |
| Negative control | Known non-sensitizer |
| Vehicle control | Extraction vehicle |

### 4.4 Acceptance Criteria

- GPMT: Sensitivity classification (Grade I–IV)
- LLNA: Stimulation index <1.6 (negative)
- No positive control failure
- Valid test system

## 5. Irritation Testing (ISO 10993-10)

### 5.1 Purpose and Scope

Irritation testing evaluates the potential of device materials to cause irritation at the site of contact.

### 5.2 Test Methods

#### 5.2.1 Intracutaneous Reactivity Test
- Extract injected intradermally
- Contralateral control with saline
- Erythema and edema scoring
- Score comparison between test and control

#### 5.2.2 Primary Irritation Test
- Topical application to skin
- Erythema and edema scoring
- Primary irritation index calculation

### 5.3 Sample Requirements

| Parameter | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Intracutaneous | 3 rabbits, 5 sites per animal |
| Primary irritation | 3 rabbits |
| Positive control | Known irritant |
| Negative control | Saline or vehicle |
| Observation period | 72 hours minimum |

### 5.4 Acceptance Criteria

- Erythema score ≤1.0 (minimal)
- Edema score ≤1.0 (minimal)
- Primary irritation index <0.5 (negligible)
- No tissue necrosis

## 6. Systemic Toxicity Testing (ISO 10993-11)

### 6.1 Purpose and Scope

Systemic toxicity testing evaluates the potential of device materials to cause systemic toxic effects through any exposure route.

### 6.2 Test Methods

#### 6.2.1 Acute Systemic Toxicity
- Single-dose exposure
- Multiple exposure routes (IV, IP, oral)
- 72-hour observation period
- Mortality and clinical signs assessment

#### 6.2.2 Subacute/Subchronic Toxicity
- Repeated dose exposure (typically 28 days)
- Multiple dose levels
- Clinical observations
- Hematology and clinical chemistry
- Necropsy and histopathology

#### 6.2.3 Chronic Toxicity
- Long-term exposure (90 days or longer)
- Multiple dose levels
- Clinical observations
- Hematology and clinical chemistry
- Necropsy and histopathology

### 6.3 Sample Requirements

| Parameter | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Acute | 5 test + 5 control per group |
| Subacute | 10 test + 10 control per group |
| Chronic | 20 test + 20 control per group |
| Positive control | Known toxic material |
| Negative control | Saline or vehicle |

### 6.4 Acceptance Criteria

- No mortality attributable to test material
- No significant clinical signs
- Hematology parameters within normal limits
- Clinical chemistry parameters within normal limits
- No treatment-related histopathological findings

## 7. Genotoxicity Testing (ISO 10993-3)

### 7.1 Purpose and Scope

Genotoxicity testing evaluates the potential of device materials to cause genetic damage, including mutations and chromosomal aberrations.

### 7.2 Test Methods

#### 7.2.1 Bacterial Reverse Mutation Assay (Ames Test)
- Salmonella typhimurium strains
- Metabolic activation (+S9 and -S9)
- Mutation frequency measurement
- Positive and negative controls

#### 7.2.2 In Vitro Mammalian Cell Assay
- Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells
- Chromosomal aberration test
- Micronucleus test
- Metabolic activation

#### 7.2.3 In Vivo Mammalian Assay
- Mouse micronucleus test
- Bone marrow sampling
- Peripheral blood sampling
- Chromosome aberration in vivo

### 7.3 Sample Requirements

| Test | Sample Size | Duration |
|------|------------|----------|
| Ames test | 5 dose levels + controls | 3–5 days |
| In vitro mammalian | 4 dose levels + controls | 24–72 hours |
| In vivo mammalian | 3 dose levels + controls | 24–72 hours |

### 7.4 Acceptance Criteria

- Ames test: Negative (<2× increase in revertants)
- In vitro mammalian: Negative (no significant increase in aberrations)
- In vivo mammalian: Negative (no significant increase in micronuclei)

## 8. Implantation Testing (ISO 10993-6)

### 8.1 Purpose and Scope

Implantation testing evaluates the local tissue response to device materials implanted in living tissue.

### 8.2 Test Methods

#### 8.2.1 Implantation Site Selection
- Muscle implantation (rabbit)
- Bone implantation (rabbit)
- Subcutaneous implantation (rabbit)
- Site selection based on device application

#### 8.2.2 Implantation Duration
- Short-term: 12 weeks
- Long-term: 26 weeks or longer
- Duration based on intended implant duration

#### 8.2.3 Tissue Response Assessment
- Histological evaluation
- Inflammation scoring
- Fibrosis scoring
- Comparison to control material

### 8.3 Sample Requirements

| Parameter | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Test articles | 10 implants per time point |
| Control articles | 10 implants per time point |
| Positive control | Known reactive material |
| Negative control | Known inert material (e.g., UHMWPE) |
| Time points | 12 weeks, 26 weeks |

### 8.4 Acceptance Criteria

- Inflammation score ≤ control material
- Fibrosis score ≤ control material
- No evidence of tissue necrosis
- No evidence of chronic inflammation
- Appropriate tissue integration

## 9. Hemocompatibility Testing (ISO 10993-4)

### 9.1 Purpose and Scope

Hemocompatibility testing evaluates the interaction of device materials with blood, including thrombosis, hemolysis, coagulation, and platelet function.

### 9.2 Test Methods

#### 9.2.1 Thrombosis Assessment
- In vivo thrombosis model
- Thrombus weight measurement
- Histological evaluation of thrombus

#### 9.2.2 Hemolysis Assessment
- In vitro hemolysis test
- Hemoglobin release measurement
- Hemolysis percentage calculation

#### 9.2.3 Coagulation Assessment
- Prothrombin time (PT)
- Activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT)
- Fibrinogen measurement
- Thrombin time

#### 9.2.4 Platelet Function Assessment
- Platelet adhesion testing
- Platelet activation markers
- Platelet aggregation

### 9.3 Sample Requirements

| Test | Sample Size | Duration |
|------|------------|----------|
| Thrombosis | 6 implants per group | 1–24 hours |
| Hemolysis | 3 extracts per group | 4 hours |
| Coagulation | 3 plasma samples per group | Immediate |
| Platelet function | 3 samples per group | 1 hour |

### 9.4 Acceptance Criteria

- Hemolysis rate <5%
- Thrombus weight ≤ control material
- Coagulation parameters within normal limits
- Platelet adhesion ≤ control material

## 10. Degradation Product Assessment

### 10.1 Purpose and Scope

Degradation product assessment identifies and quantifies potential degradation products from device materials during implantation.

### 10.2 Assessment Framework

Per ISO 10993-9, the assessment includes:

1. Material characterization
2. Degradation mechanism identification
3. Degradation product identification
4. Degradation product quantification
5. Toxicological risk assessment

### 10.3 Material-Specific Assessment

#### 10.3.1 Polymers (ISO 10993-13)
- Extractable and leachable analysis
- Oxidative degradation products
- Hydrolytic degradation products
- Additive degradation products

#### 10.3.2 Metals (ISO 10993-15)
- Ion release testing
- Corrosion product analysis
- Alloy degradation products
- Surface treatment effects

#### 10.3.3 Ceramics (ISO 10993-14)
- Dissolution testing
- Particulate analysis
- Ion release assessment

### 10.4 Toxicological Risk Assessment

Per ISO 10993-17:

- Identify all degradation products
- Establish tolerable exposure levels
- Compare actual exposure to tolerable levels
- Assess overall risk
- Document findings in risk assessment report

## 11. Chemical Characterization (ISO 10993-18)

### 11.1 Purpose and Scope

Chemical characterization identifies and quantifies chemical constituents of device materials, including extractables and leachables.

### 11.2 Testing Methods

#### 11.2.1 Extractable Analysis
- Organic extractables (GC-MS, LC-MS)
- Inorganic extractables (ICP-MS, ICP-OES)
- Non-volatile residues
- Volatile organic compounds

#### 11.2.2 Leachable Analysis
- Simulated extraction conditions
- Physiologically based extraction
- Accelerated extraction

### 11.3 Sample Preparation

- Extraction conditions per ISO 10993-12
- Extraction vehicles (polar and non-polar)
- Extraction temperatures and durations
- Surface area to volume ratios

### 11.4 Acceptance Criteria

- All extractables identified and quantified
- Tolerable exposure levels established
- Toxicological risk assessment completed
- Comparison to established limits

## 12. Biological Risk Assessment

### 12.1 Risk Assessment Process

The biological risk assessment for the iPACE-CHIP follows ISO 10993-1 and ISO 14971:

1. **Material Identification:** Identify all patient-contacting materials
2. **Contact Classification:** Classify nature and duration of contact
3. **Existing Data Review:** Review available biocompatibility data
4. **Testing Gap Analysis:** Identify additional testing needs
5. **Testing Execution:** Perform required testing
6. **Results Interpretation:** Interpret test results in context
7. **Risk Evaluation:** Evaluate overall biological risk
8. **Benefit-Risk Analysis:** Compare benefits to residual risks

### 12.2 Risk Assessment Documentation

The biological risk assessment must be documented in a Biological Evaluation Report (BER) that includes:

- Material characterization summary
- Contact classification
- Review of existing data
- Testing plan and rationale
- Test results summary
- Risk evaluation
- Conclusions and recommendations

## 13. Special Considerations for iPACE-CHIP

### 13.1 Long-Term Implantation

For devices implanted for >30 days, additional considerations include:

- Chronic inflammation assessment
- Fibrosis and encapsulation
- Long-term degradation product accumulation
- Cumulative toxic effects

### 13.2 Blood-Contacting Surfaces

For lead surfaces contacting blood:

- Thrombogenicity assessment
- Hemolysis evaluation
- Coagulation cascade interaction
- Platelet function effects

### 13.3 Multiple Material Combinations

The iPACE-CHIP contains multiple materials requiring:

- Material compatibility assessment
- Galvanic corrosion evaluation
- Combined material testing
- Worst-case material combinations

### 13.4 Sterilization Effects

Sterilization may affect biocompatibility:

- Ethylene oxide residue testing
- Gamma irradiation effects on materials
- Autoclave effects on materials
- Sterilization validation per ISO 11135 or ISO 11137

## 14. Testing Laboratory Requirements

### 14.1 Accreditation

Testing laboratories must be:

- ISO 17025 accredited (preferred)
- GLP compliant (required for FDA submissions)
- CAP certified (if clinical testing)
- Regularly audited

### 14.2 Quality Controls

- Positive and negative controls
- Validated test methods
- Calibration and maintenance
- Proficiency testing
- Data integrity requirements

## 15. Conclusion

ISO 10993 compliance is essential for the iPACE-CHIP implantable pacemaker to demonstrate that all patient-contacting materials are safe for long-term implantation. The comprehensive testing framework, combined with rigorous material characterization and biological risk assessment, provides the evidence necessary for regulatory submissions worldwide. Integration with ISO 14971 risk management ensures that biocompatibility risks are properly identified, evaluated, and controlled.
