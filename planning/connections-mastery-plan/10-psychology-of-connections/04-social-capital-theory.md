# Social Capital Theory

## Understanding the Currency of Networks

### What is Social Capital?

Social capital is the value embedded in your network. It's not what you know or who you are — it's who you know and who knows you.

**Three types of social capital:**

**1. Bonding Social Capital**
- Deep connections within your group
- Trust, solidarity, mutual support
- "People like us"
- Example: Your inner circle, close allies, family

**2. Bridging Social Capital**
- Connections across different groups
- New information, new opportunities
- "People different from us"
- Example: Cross-domain connections, diverse network

**3. Linking Social Capital**
- Connections to people in power/authority
- Access to resources, influence, institutional power
- "People above us"
- Example: Government officials, executives, institutional leaders

### Your Social Capital Portfolio

| Type | Current Status | Needed For |
|------|---------------|------------|
| Bonding | Low (starting) | Support, trust, reliability |
| Bridging | Developing (your focus) | Information, opportunities, growth |
| Linking | Zero (starting) | Access, influence, power |

**Your strategy:** Build bridging capital first (your AI expertise opens doors), then bonding (deepen key relationships), then linking (climb the power ladder).

### How Social Capital Compounds

Each connection increases the value of all other connections because:
- **Network effects:** More people = more possible connections between them
- **Information flow:** More diverse info sources = better decisions
- **Brokerage opportunities:** More gaps between your connections = more value you can create by bridging them

**Metcalfe's Law for Networks:** The value of a network grows exponentially with the number of nodes (connections).

### Measuring Social Capital

**Quantitative metrics:**
- Network size (total connections)
- Network density (how many of your connections know each other)
- Network diversity (number of domains, industries, geographies)
- Centrality (how many shortest paths go through you)

**Qualitative metrics:**
- Trust level (would they vouch for you?)
- Reciprocity (is value flow balanced?)
- Access (can you reach key people?)
- Influence (can you mobilize your network?)

### Building Social Capital

**Bonding capital:**
- Regular communication with inner circle
- Shared experiences and memories
- Mutual support during crises
- Deep trust built over time

**Bridging capital:**
- Entering new communities and domains
- Attending diverse events
- Making cross-domain introductions
- Learning different professional languages

**Linking capital:**
- Building relationships with people in power
- Demonstrating value to decision-makers
- Getting introduced through trusted channels
- Developing reputation that opens doors

### Social Capital in Different Cultures

**High-context cultures (Japan, Middle East, India):**
- Relationships come before business
- Trust built slowly through social interaction
- Bonding capital is essential
- Linking capital requires personal vouching

**Low-context cultures (USA, Germany, Scandinavia):**
- Business can happen before relationships
- Trust built through competence and reliability
- Bridging capital is valued
- Linking capital can be more transactional

**Your approach:** Adapt to the culture you're operating in. When in doubt, invest in relationship first.

### Preserving Social Capital

Social capital can be destroyed:

**Social capital destroyers:**
1. Breaking trust or promises
2. Using connections for short-term gain
3. Being a taker (always asking, never giving)
4. Gossiping or betraying confidences
5. Letting relationships go dormant
6. Burning bridges when leaving a situation

**Preservation strategies:**
- Regular maintenance touches
- Generosity without expectation
- Protecting others' reputations
- Staying in touch even when you don't need anything

### Converting Social Capital

Social capital can be converted to other forms of capital:

- **Financial capital:** Deals, investments, job offers, clients
- **Human capital:** Knowledge, skills, mentorship
- **Cultural capital:** Status, reputation, prestige
- **Political capital:** Influence, access to power

**The conversion rate** depends on your skill at identifying and facilitating opportunities.

### The Social Capital Flywheel

```
Build relationships → Create value → Gain reputation
    ↓
More people seek you → More opportunities → More value creation
    ↓
Deeper relationships → More trust → More access
    ↓
Higher status → More inbound → More options
    ↓
Compound effects → Exponential growth
```

### Your Social Capital Goals

**Year 1:** Build bridging capital (wide, diverse network)
**Year 2:** Strengthen bonding capital (deep, trusted relationships)
**Year 3:** Develop linking capital (access to power and influence)
**Year 4+:** All three types strong, compounding together
