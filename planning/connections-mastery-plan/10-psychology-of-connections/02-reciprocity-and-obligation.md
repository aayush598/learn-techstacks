# Reciprocity and Obligation

## The Currency of Relationships

### The Reciprocity Instinct

Humans are wired to return favors. This is universal across cultures. When someone does something for us, we feel psychological discomfort until we've done something in return.

This instinct is your greatest ally in networking — when used ethically.

### The Reciprocity Cycle

```
You give value → They feel grateful
    ↓
They feel obligation → They look for ways to reciprocate
    ↓
They give value → You feel grateful
    ↓
You look for ways to reciprocate → Cycle continues
```

### Give First, Always

The golden rule of networking reciprocity:

**Never ask for something until you've given something first.**

Even if it's small. Even if they didn't ask for it. Even if they don't immediately reciprocate.

### Types of Reciprocal Value

**Immediate reciprocity:**
- They give you advice, you thank them publicly
- They introduce you, you introduce them to someone
- They share your content, you share theirs

**Delayed reciprocity:**
- You help them today, they help you months later
- You connect them, they remember when you need connections
- You support their project, they support yours in the future

**Unexpected reciprocity (most powerful):**
- You help someone who can't possibly return the favor
- Someone else sees this and offers to help you
- Your reputation for generosity attracts value

### Creating Reciprocity (Ethically)

**1. Give with no strings attached**
- True generosity triggers the deepest reciprocity
- If it comes with conditions, it's not generosity

**2. Give specifically and personally**
- A generic resource shows less care than something tailored
- Personal effort increases the sense of obligation

**3. Give first in the relationship**
- The first move of generosity sets the tone
- You create a positive imbalance they'll want to correct

**4. Give in areas that matter to them**
- A small help on something they care about > a large help on something they don't

### Managing Obligation

**Never keep score.** But do track:

- What have I given to this relationship?
- What have they given to me?
- Is there an imbalance? Who owes whom?

If you've given much more than received, you have "credit" — but don't call it in aggressively. Let them offer.

If you've received much more than given, find ways to rebalance.

### The Danger of Over-Obligation

If you give too much too fast without letting them reciprocate, they may:
- Feel uncomfortable and avoid you
- Feel indebted and resentful
- Question your motives

**Balance:** Give generously but allow them to reciprocate naturally. Don't force them into your debt.

### Reciprocity by Relationship Stage

| Stage | Giving | Receiving |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| First contact | Give small value (resource, insight) | Accept their time/attention |
| Early relationship | Give medium value (intro, feedback) | Accept medium help |
| Established relationship | Give large value (strategic help) | Accept large help |
| Strategic alliance | Mutual, ongoing exchange | Balanced, continual |

### When Reciprocity Doesn't Happen

Some people take without giving. They are:
- Oblivious (don't notice the imbalance)
- Entitled (expect others to serve them)
- Overwhelmed (intend to reciprocate but don't)

**Your response:**
1. Don't give more than you're comfortable losing
2. Politely stop giving and see if they notice
3. If they're genuinely oblivious, a gentle reminder may help
4. If they're entitled, reduce the relationship to surface level

### The Ultimate Reciprocity Truth

The most powerful form of reciprocity is not transactional but relational. When you build genuine relationships, reciprocity happens naturally — not because of obligation, but because people who care about you want to see you succeed.
