## Becoming a Go-To Source and Media Personality

### The Power of Being a Media Source

Every time a journalist quotes you in an article, you get two things: credibility and reach. Credibility because the publication vetted you. Reach because thousands of influential people read that article.

Your goal is to become a "regular source" for 5-10 journalists covering AI, tech, and healthcare. When they need an expert quote, they should think of you first.

|---|---|---|

**How to Engage with Journalists on X:**

1. **Don't just retweet**: Add your own commentary
2. **Quote-tweet with insight**: "This is an important finding. Our research shows [related point]. [Link to your work]"
3. **Reply to their polls**: Many journalists run polls
4. **Mention them in relevant threads**: When you write something related to their beat

**Sample Engagement:**

>
> You: "Important point. Our analysis of 200 AI startups shows only 12% currently track data provenance. This will be a major compliance challenge. Curious to see implementation timelines."

### Providing Value as a Source/Expert

**The Good Source Protocol:**

| Quality | What You Do |
|---|---|
| Responsive | Reply to journalist texts/calls within 1 hour |
| Quotable | Speak in short, clear, opinionated sentences |
| Fast | Provide quotes within 2-4 hours of request |
| Visual | Have charts, diagrams, demos ready |
| Relatable | Connect complex topics to everyday life |
| Honest | Say "I don't know" when you don't |
| Prepared | Have 3-5 reusable anecdotes/quotes ready |
| Respectful | Respect their deadline, word count, angle |

**Creating Your Source Package:**

Prepare these materials in advance:

1. **Bio (50 words)**:
   > "X is an AI engineer specialising in [domain]. He has [X] years of experience building [type of systems] and has been published in [journals/publications]."

2. **Bio (100 words)**:
   > [More detailed version, including specific achievements]

3. **3-5 Reusable Quotes**:
   > - "AI in India is like a bicycle—it's not replacing cars, but it's the most practical solution for most people."
   > - "The biggest AI challenge in India isn't technology; it's data quality."
   > - "Indian companies are leapfrogging legacy systems straight into AI-first architecture."

4. **5 Reusable Data Points**:
   > - "India has X AI startups, growing at Y% annually."
   > - "Only Z% of Indian hospitals use AI in any form."
   > - "The average cost of [AI solution] has dropped by W% in 2 years."

5. **1-Page Backgrounder**:
   > A PDF with your bio, areas of expertise, past media mentions, and contact info

**What Not to Do:**

| Don't | Why |
|---|---|
| Ask to approve the article | Journalists won't work with you again |
| Send 500-word emails | They don't have time |
| Pitch irrelevant stories | You lose credibility |
| Get angry if not quoted | Sent happens |
| Name-drop other journalists | Looks desperate |
| Ask to "get coffee" | They're too busy (offer a 10-min call) |
| Call without texting first | Always text to check timing |

### Writing Op-Eds and Guest Articles

An op-ed (opposite the editorial page) is your chance to state your views in your own words.

**Op-Ed Structure:**

| Section | Content | Length |
|---|---|---|
| Hook | Grab attention with a story, data point, or question | 2-3 sentences |
| Problem | What's wrong with the current situation | 2-3 paragraphs |
| Your Solution | What should be done differently | 2-3 paragraphs |
| Evidence | Why your solution works (data, examples) | 1-2 paragraphs |
| Call to Action | What needs to happen next | 1 paragraph |
| Credibility Line | Your bio | 1 sentence |

**Op-Ed Example (AI in Indian Elections):**

> **Headline**: "India Needs AI to Fight Political Misinformation"
>
> [Hook] The 2024 election saw 20 million fake videos circulate on WhatsApp. Next time, it could be 200 million.
>
> [Problem] AI-generated deepfakes are becoming indistinguishable from reality. India's 900 million voters are vulnerable. The current response—fact-checking after content goes viral—is too slow.
>
> [Solution] We need AI-powered real-time detection systems that flag suspicious content before it spreads. Our team built a prototype that identifies deepfakes with 94% accuracy in under 30 seconds.
>
> [Evidence] In a pilot with 1,000 WhatsApp groups during the state elections, our system flagged 40% of political content as potentially manipulated. Manual verification confirmed 85% of flags were accurate.
>
> [Call to Action] The Election Commission should partner with AI researchers to deploy these systems for the 2029 elections. The technology exists. The will needs to follow.
>
> [Bio] X is an AI engineer specialising in computer vision and media forensics. He built India's first deepfake detection API.

**What Makes a Good Podcast Guest:**

1. **Energy**: Podcasts need vocal energy. Practice speaking with enthusiasm.
2. **Storytelling**: Have personal anecdotes ready
3. **Conciseness**: Answer questions in 30-60 seconds, not 5 minutes
4. **Controversial Takes**: "I actually disagree with the popular view..."
5. **Value for the Audience**: Can listeners learn something useful?

**Preparing for a Podcast Appearance:**

| Preparation | Action |
|---|---|
| Listen to 3 episodes | Understand the host's style |
| Prepare 5 key points | Write them out |
| Prepare 3 stories | Personal, professional, interesting |
| Know your stats | Key data points memorised |
| Tech check | Good microphone, quiet room |
| Promote | Share episode with your network |
| Follow up | Thank the host, share feedback |

**YouTube Appearances:**

YouTube is different from podcasts—visual elements matter.

- Have a good webcam (or go to a studio)
- Prepare visual aids (slides, demos, screen recordings)
- Dress professionally (solid colours, no busy patterns)
- Look at the camera (not the screen)
- Pause, don't rush

### Building Relationships with Tech Journalists

Long-term relationships beat one-off pitches.

**The Relationship Building System:**

**Phase 1: Observer (Week 1-2)**
- Follow them on X, LinkedIn
- Read their last 10 articles
- Understand their style, beat, preferences
- Like/share/comment meaningfully (not "Great article!")

**Phase 2: Value Provider (Week 3-4)**
- Send a relevant study/data point: "Saw your piece on deepfakes. Here's a new dataset from IIT that might interest you."
- Offer to introduce them to someone: "I know a researcher working on this exact topic."
- Send a tip: "Heard that [Company] is launching [product] next week."

**Phase 3: Expert Source (Month 2)**
- When they're writing about your area, offer to help
- "I'm working on something relevant to your next piece. Happy to offer a quote or context."
- Be available quickly when they need you

**Phase 4: Trusted Relationship (Month 3+)**
- They reach out to you proactively
- You get cited in their articles regularly
- You get invited to their events
- You meet off the record for background briefings

**Maintaining the Relationship:**

| Frequency | Action |
|---|---|
| Weekly | Share useful content (without pitching) |
| Monthly | Ping to check in, offer help |
| Quarterly | Propose a story idea |
| Yearly | Meet in person (coffee, drink) |
| Milestones | Congratulate on their achievements |

### Final Words

Becoming a media source is one of the highest-leverage activities you can do for your network. One quote in The Times of India can get you more credibility than a year of self-promotion. One op-ed can establish you as a thought leader.

But it requires patience. Journalists are suspicious of self-promoters. They want experts who genuinely help, not people who just want publicity. Be the helper. Provide value without expecting anything in return. Over time, the recognition and access will come.

Your AI expertise is rare and valuable in the media world. Most journalists don't know what a transformer model is, or why GPT-4 is different from GPT-3.5. You can translate tech for them. That's a skill worth more than any degree.

Be the person journalists call when they need to understand AI. Their Rolodex becomes yours.
