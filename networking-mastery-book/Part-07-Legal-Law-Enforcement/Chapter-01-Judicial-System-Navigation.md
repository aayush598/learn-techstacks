## The Indian Judicial System: A Quick Overview

### The Court Hierarchy

**Supreme Court of India:**
- Highest court, final court of appeal
- Located in New Delhi (Tilak Marg)
- Chief Justice of India (CJI): Head of the judiciary
- 34 judges (including CJI)
- Jurisdiction: Constitutional matters, appeals from High Courts, public interest
- Current CJI (2026): [Current office holder]

**High Courts:**
- 25 High Courts across states
- Each state has its own High Court (some states share)
- Some states have multiple benches (e.g., Bombay High Court has benches in Nagpur, Aurangabad)
- Headed by a Chief Justice
- Jurisdiction: Appeals from district courts, writ petitions, constitutional matters

**District & Sessions Courts:**
- One in each district (700+ districts in India)
- Headed by a District & Sessions Judge
- Civil matters (District Judge) and Criminal matters (Sessions Judge)
- Subordinate courts: Civil Judges, Magistrates, Munsifs

**Tribunals:**
- Specialized courts for specific matters
- NCLT (National Company Law Tribunal) - corporate cases
- ITAT (Income Tax Appellate Tribunal) - tax cases
- CAT (Central Administrative Tribunal) - government service matters
- SAT (Securities Appellate Tribunal) - market-related cases
- Debts Recovery Tribunal (DRT) - banking recovery
- Green Tribunal (NGT) - environmental cases

### Key People in the System

**Supreme Court:**
- Chief Justice of India
- Justices (34 total)
- Registrar General (administrative head)
- Additional Registrars

**High Court:**
- Chief Justice
- Justices
- Registrar General
- Additional Registrars

**District Court:**
- District & Sessions Judge
- Additional District Judges
- Chief Judicial Magistrate (CJM)
- Civil Judges and Judicial Magistrates

## Understanding Legal Hierarchy and Power Structure

### The Appointment System

**Supreme Court Judges:**
- Appointed by the President of India
- Based on recommendations from the Collegium (CJI + 4 senior-most SC judges)
- Usually from High Court judges with 10+ years of service
- Retire at age 65

**High Court Judges:**
- Appointed by the President
- Based on Collegium recommendations
- Usually from District Court judges or advocates with 10+ years practice
- Retire at age 62

**District Judges:**
- Appointed by the Governor of the state
- Through competitive examination (for direct recruits) or promotion
- Retire at age 60

### The Bar (Lawyers) Hierarchy

**Senior Advocates:**
- Designated by High Courts and Supreme Court
- Experienced advocates (20+ years)
- Highest-paid, most influential
- Usually don't take direct briefs from clients (work through juniors)

**Advocates on Record (AOR):**
- Special category for Supreme Court
- Must pass AOR exam
- Can file documents in Supreme Court
- Other advocates must work through an AOR

**Regular Advocates:**
- Enrolled with State Bar Council
- Practice in District Courts, High Courts
- 1.7 million+ registered advocates in India

**Junior Advocates:**
- Fresh graduates, 0-5 years experience
- Work as assistants to senior advocates
- Low pay (₹10,000-30,000/month initially)
- Most accessible for networking

## Courtroom Etiquette and Observation Opportunities

### How to Observe Court Proceedings

Court proceedings in India are generally open to the public. You can walk into most courts and watch.

**Supreme Court:**
- Open to public
- Courtrooms 1-5 are the main constitutional benches
- Entry is free but security checks are strict
- Bring government ID (Aadhaar, passport, etc.)
- Dress code: Formal wear (shirt, trousers - no jeans/t-shirts)
- Best days: Monday-Wednesday (constitutional matters heard)

**High Court:**
- Open to public
- Multiple courtrooms functioning simultaneously
- More relaxed security
- Dress code: Formal (advocates wear black coats, visitors need decent formals)

**District Court:**
- Most accessible
- Security is minimal
- You can sit in the audience area
- Observe how lawyers present arguments

### Courtroom Protocol

**Do's:**
- Enter quietly and sit in the designated visitor area
- Stand when the judge enters
- Maintain silence during proceedings
- Take notes (shows you're serious)
- Leave when the court rises

**Don'ts:**
- Don't eat, drink, or use your phone
- Don't interrupt proceedings
- Don't approach the judge's dais
- Don't take photos (strictly prohibited)
- Don't talk to lawyers during arguments

### What to Observe

When you sit in a court:

1. **How arguments are structured:**
   "My Lord, the issue before this Hon'ble Court is..."
   Notice the language, structure, and citations

2. **How judges interact:**
   Notice what questions they ask, what they focus on

3. **How lawyers interact:**
   Notice how they present facts, cite cases, respond to counter-arguments

4. **Court procedures:**
   How cases are called, how evidence is submitted, how orders are passed

### Finding What Cases Are Being Heard

**Cause Lists:**
- Every court publishes a "cause list" daily
- Lists all cases being heard that day
- Available on court websites
- Download and find cases that interest you

**Supreme Court:**
- supremecourtofindia.nic.in → Daily Cause List
- Cases listed by court number
- Constitutional cases, special leave petitions, regular hearings

**High Court:**
- Each High Court's website has cause lists
- Check the day before you plan to visit

## Legal Terminology and Basic Law Knowledge

### Essential Legal Terms

**Must-Know for Court Observation:**

- **Petitioner/Appellant**: Person filing the case/appeal
- **Respondent**: Person defending the case
- **Counsel**: Lawyer (Counsel for the Petitioner, etc.)
- **Writ**: Court order to do or not do something
- **Petition**: Written application to the court
- **Affidavit**: Sworn written statement
- **Summons**: Court order to appear
- **Stay**: Court order stopping an action
- **Interim Order**: Temporary order until final decision
- **Decree**: Final court order
- **Ratio Decidendi**: The legal reasoning behind a judgment
- **Obiter Dicta**: Comments made by the judge (not binding)

**Indian Legal Acts You Should Know:**

- Indian Constitution (fundamental rights, directive principles)
- Indian Penal Code, 1860 (IPC - criminal law, being replaced by BNS)
- Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (CrPC - being replaced by BNSS)
- Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (CPC)
- Indian Evidence Act, 1872 (being replaced by BSA)
- Information Technology Act, 2000 (cyber laws, relevant to you)
- Companies Act, 2013 (corporate law)
- Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996

### How to Learn Basic Law Without Law School

**Online Resources:**
- Legal Bites (free legal education content)
- Bar Council of India's online resources
- NALSA (National Legal Services Authority) - free legal literacy materials
- Supreme Court's website (judgments are freely available)
- Indian Kanoon (largest database of Indian judgments, FREE)
- SCC Online (subscription needed, most comprehensive)
- Manupatra (similar to SCC Online)

**YouTube Channels for Legal Knowledge:**
- Study IQ Judiciary (free legal education)

**Books for Basic Legal Literacy:**

## Public Interest Litigation (PIL) as Networking Entry

### What is PIL?

Public Interest Litigation allows any citizen to approach the court on matters of public importance. You don't need to be personally affected.

**Examples of PILs:**
- Environmental protection (pollution in Delhi, Ganga cleaning)
- Corruption in public office
- Violation of fundamental rights
- Ensuring implementation of laws
- Police reforms, prison reforms

### How PIL Connects You

Filing or supporting a PIL involves:
- Meeting lawyers who specialize in PIL
- Engaging with social activists
- Appearing before judges (if you argue your own case)
- Getting media coverage (PILs are often covered)
- Connecting with other citizens who care about the issue

### Using Your AI Skills for PIL

You can:
1. Use AI to analyze data and identify violations of rights
2. Build AI agents that monitor compliance with court orders
3. Create automated legal research tools for PIL advocates
4. Help document evidence for PIL cases (using AI for document analysis)

**Real Example:** A computer science student in Delhi used AI to analyze air quality data and filed a PIL through a senior advocate about Delhi's pollution. The case got media coverage, the student was interviewed by news channels, and connected with environmental lawyers and activists across India.

### The PIL Networking Path

1. Identify an issue you care about (environment, education, public health, technology rights)
2. Research existing PILs on the topic
3. Connect with the lawyers who filed those PILs
4. Offer your AI skills to help with data analysis or documentation
5. Follow the case and engage with the legal team
6. Attend court hearings and observe the process

## Courtroom Observation and Interactions

### Building Relationships with Court Staff

Court staff are the gatekeepers of the legal system. They can help you navigate the system.

**People to Know:**
- Registry/Administrative staff (they manage case listings)
- Court Masters (assist the judge in the courtroom)
- Stenographers (they record proceedings)
- Librarians (court libraries have amazing resources)

**How to Connect:**
- Be polite and respectful (court staff are often undervalued)
- Visit regularly and greet them by name
- Ask questions about court procedures (ask for their help)
- Share food/tea during breaks (small gestures matter)

### Networking with Lawyers in Court

**Before Court:**
- Arrive early (lawyers gather outside courtrooms before 10 AM)
- Stand near the bar (the designated area for lawyers in court)
- Listen to their conversations (they discuss cases)
- Interject politely: "Sorry to interrupt - I'm a technology professional interested in legal AI. May I ask what case you're working on?"

**During Break:**
- Lawyers gather in the break room, canteen, or library
- Approach with: "I'm visiting court to understand the legal process. Can I ask what area of law you practice?"
- Transition to: "I work with AI and technology. Have you considered using AI for your legal research?"

**After Court:**
- Connect on LinkedIn: "Great meeting you at [Court Name]. I was impressed by your argument on [Case]. Would love to learn more about your work."
- Follow up with relevant resources (AI tools for lawyers)

## Supreme Court and High Court Visitor Policies

### Supreme Court Visitor Rules

**Timings:**
- Court hours: 10:30 AM to 4:00 PM (Monday-Friday)
- Cause list published at 5:00 PM for the next day
- Constitution benches usually sit on Monday-Wednesday

**Entry:**
- Free and open to public
- Entry through Gate No. 1 (main gate) on Tilak Marg
- Security check (metal detector, bag check)
- No electronic devices in courtrooms (phones must be switched off)

**Dress Code:**
- Men: Formal shirt and trousers, closed shoes
- Women: Formal salwar-kameez or saree
- NO: Jeans, t-shirts, shorts, flip-flops

**What to See:**
- Courtroom 1 (CJI's court) - Constitution bench matters
- Courtroom 2-5 - Other constitution benches
- Courtroom 6+ - Division benches and single judges
- Supreme Court Museum - history of Indian judiciary
- Supreme Court Library - open to public (requires visitor pass)

### High Court Visitor Rules

**Access:**
- Generally more relaxed than Supreme Court
- No special pass needed for entry
- Multiple courtrooms, you can walk between them

**Best Time to Visit:**
- 10:30 AM to 1:00 PM (morning session, fresh arguments)
- 2:00 PM to 4:00 PM (afternoon session, orders and minor matters)
- Monday is the busiest day

### District Court Visitor Rules

**Access:**
- Completely open to public
- No security check in many district courts
- You can literally walk into any courtroom

**Best Time:**
- Morning hours (10:30-1:00) are most active
- Criminal courts and civil courts running simultaneously

## Using RTI as a Legal Networking Tool

### What is RTI?

The Right to Information Act, 2005 gives every citizen the right to ask questions to government departments. Public authorities must respond within 30 days.

### How RTI Connects You to Legal Circles

**Using RTI to:**
1. Understand how courts function (ask about case pendency, judge vacancies)
2. Get information about legal aid programs (from NALSA/DLSA)
3. Access judicial orders and their implementation status
4. Monitor compliance with court orders

**Connections from RTI:**
- RTI activists are often lawyers or legally literate
- Filing RTIs brings you in contact with government officials
- Appeals (first appeal, second appeal to SIC) bring you before quasi-judicial officers

**Your AI + RTI Advantage:**
Use AI to:
- Draft RTI applications (they require specific wording)
- Analyze responses from multiple RTIs
- Identify patterns in government functioning
- Create public databases from RTI-obtained information
