# Chapter 2: Diplomatic Corps Networking — Connecting with Embassies, Diplomats, and Foreign Service

## 2.1 Why Diplomatic Networking Matters for a Tech Person

Imagine this: You're at the Raisina Dialogue in New Delhi. You strike up a conversation with the First Secretary (Economic) from the US Embassy. You mention you're building AI agents for supply chain optimization. She thinks of the US-India Commercial Dialogue happening next month, where they need success stories. She introduces you to the Commercial Counselor. Three months later, your startup is featured at a US-India business forum with 200+

companies attending.

This is not fiction. This is how diplomatic networking works.

Diplomats are **multipliers.** One diplomat knows:
- Business delegations from their country
- Investors looking for opportunities in India
- Journalists covering India
- Academics, think tankers, and policy experts
- Visa officers who can facilitate travel
- Political leaders and their advisors

**Your job:** Become the "AI person" that diplomats know. When they need an expert for a panel, a startup for a delegation, or a quote for a media article, your LinkedIn ping arrives.

### How to Get on the Guest List

**Strategy 1: Event Aggregators**
- 10times.com — search "embassy" or "diplomatic"
- Eventbrite — embassies increasingly use this
- Delhi Events — delhievents.com
- LBB (Little Black Book) — Delhi/NCR events

**Strategy 2: Embassy Newsletters**
Every embassy has a mailing list. Sign up on their website. Events are announced via email.

**Strategy 3: LinkedIn Events**
Follow embassies on LinkedIn. They post events as LinkedIn Events. Click "Interested" — you'll get notified.

**Strategy 4: Cold Email (Works Better Than You Think)**

Template:
"Dear [Name at Embassy],
I'm [your name], an AI engineer working on [specific domain]. I've been following the work of [Embassy Name] in Tech/Innovation/Business. I would be grateful to attend [event name] on [date]. I believe my work on [specific project] aligns with [Embassy's priority area]. Thank you for considering this request."

Send to: embassy's public affairs/cultural section, or events email.

**Strategy 5: Volunteer**
Embassies need volunteers for events. Registration, ushering, social media coverage. Volunteer once, get known, get invited to everything.

**Strategy 6: Partner Organizations**
- FICCI, CII, ASSOCHAM (industry bodies) co-host events with embassies
- Think tanks (ORF, VIF, IDSA) co-host policy dialogues

Join these partner orgs as a member or participant to access their event invites.

## 2.4 Connecting with Diplomats and Foreign Service Officers

### Understanding Diplomatic Ranks

| Rank | Seniority | Who They Are | How to Address |
|------|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| Ambassador/High Commissioner | Head of Mission | Represents the head of state | "Your Excellency" then "Mr./Madam Ambassador" |
| Deputy Head of Mission | #2 | Runs the embassy | "Mr./Ms. [Name]" |
| Minister/Counselor | Senior | Heads a section (Political, Economic, Consular) | "Mr./Ms. [Name]" |
| First Secretary | Mid-level | Deputy section head | "Mr./Ms. [Name]" |
| Second Secretary | Junior | Section officer | "Mr./Ms. [Name]" |
| Third Secretary | Entry level | Junior officer | "[First name]" in informal settings |
| Attaché | Specialized | Defense, commercial, cultural, etc. | "Mr./Ms. [Name]" |

### Where to Find Them

**1. Think Tank Events**
ORF, VIF, IDSA, CPR — diplomats regularly attend as panelists or audience. Arrive early. Stay late. They're often more approachable after the formal session.

**2. Business Chambers**
FICCI, CII, ASSOCHAM events have strong diplomatic attendance. The "CII-UK" or "FICCI-USA" sessions specifically.

**3. University Lectures**
Foreign diplomats give guest lectures at Delhi University, JNU, IIFT, IIT Delhi. Attend. Ask a question during Q&A. Approach after.

**4. Cultural Centers**
- British Council (Delhi, Mumbai, Chennai, Kolkata)
- American Center (Delhi, Mumbai, Chennai, Kolkata)
- Max Mueller Bhavan/Goethe Institut (German, multiple cities)
- Alliance Française (French, multiple cities)
- Instituto Cervantes (Spanish, Delhi)

Become a regular. Attend their events. Join their language classes. You'll naturally meet diplomats.

**5. Industry Events**
- India Mobile Congress
- BioAsia
- Global AI Summit
- Startup Mahakumbh

Embassies send their commercial section to these events. Target the "International Pavilion."

### How to Approach a Diplomat

**The Wrong Way:**
"Sir, I admire your country. Please give me a visa. I want a job. Can you help?"

**The Right Way:**

At a panel discussion. Diplomat finishes speaking on "US-India Tech Partnership." You approach:

"Hi [Name], thank you for that talk. I found your point about [specific point] very insightful. I'm building an AI system for [domain], and your perspective on [related aspect] gave me a new angle to think about it."

They respond. You continue:
"Are you open to connecting on LinkedIn? I'd love to share my project with you and hear your thoughts."

**Key Principles:**
1. **Show you've done your homework** — reference their country, their specific work
2. **Offer value** — you're an AI expert, they might need expert input
3. **Be concise** — diplomats are busy, have a max 2-minute pitch
4. **Don't ask for anything in the first interaction** — build relationship first
5. **Follow up with relevant value** — "Since we spoke, I finished [project]. Thought you'd find it interesting for your work on [topic]."

## 2.5 Indian Foreign Service (IFS) — Understanding and Networking

### Why IFS Officers Are Valuable Connections

IFS officers are India's diplomats. They serve in 180+ Indian missions abroad. They know everyone — politicians, business leaders, media, other diplomats. One IFS officer can open doors across continents.

### IFS Entry Points

**1. IFS Association**
The IFS Association (ifsa.in) holds events. They have a "Young Diplomats" forum. These are not exclusively for IFS officers — associates sometimes attend.

**2. Public Lectures by IFS Officers**
Former ambassadors regularly speak at:
- India International Centre (IIC), Delhi
- IIC Annex, Lodhi Road, Delhi
- Sapru House (ICWA — Indian Council of World Affairs)
- JNU (School of International Studies)

**3. Sushma Swaraj Institute of Foreign Service (formerly FSI)**
The training academy for IFS officers. Located in New Delhi. They sometimes hold open events, cultural programs, and graduation ceremonies.

**4. Retired Ambassadors**
More accessible than serving officers. Many write columns, author books, speak at think tanks, and mentor young people.

**Retired Ambassadors to Connect With:**
- Shivshankar Menon (Ex-NSA, Ex-Foreign Secretary)
- S. Jaishankar (External Affairs Minister, Ex-Foreign Secretary)
- Navtej Sarna (Ex-Ambassador to US, now author)
- T.P. Sreenivasan (Ex-Ambassador, prolific writer)
- Rajiv Sikri (Ex-Secretary, author of "Challenge and Strategy")
- Kanwal Sibal (Ex-Foreign Secretary)
- Arundhati Ghose (Ex-Ambassador, known for WTO walkout)
- Hardeep Singh Puri (now Minister, Ex-Ambassador to UN)

**How to Connect:**
- Follow on Twitter/X — they are quite active
- Attend their book launches (most write books post-retirement)
- Attend think tank events where they speak
- LinkedIn (some are active, many join post-retirement)

**The IFS Officer's Perspective (What They Care About):**
- India's image abroad (national interest)
- Cultural diplomacy
- Economic diplomacy (trade, investment)
- Consular services for Indians abroad
- International law and norms

**Frame your pitch as:** "I want to contribute to India's technology diplomacy. How can AI strengthen India's standing globally?"

## 2.9 Track II Diplomacy and Think Tanks

### What is Track II Diplomacy?

Track I is official government-to-government diplomacy. Track II is unofficial — think tanks, academics, retired officials, and civil society engaging across borders to build understanding.

**Why it matters:** Track II is where you, as a non-diplomat, can participate. It's less formal, more substantive, and equally impactful.

### Major Indian Think Tanks

### How to Use Think Tanks for Networking

**Step 1:** Sign up for mailing lists of 5 think tanks

## 2.11 Building Relationships with Embassy Staff

### The Embassy Ecosystem

An embassy is not just diplomats. It's also:
- **Local staff** — Indian nationals who work at embassies (often *more* powerful for networking than diplomats because they stay for years and know everyone)
- **Cultural staff** — program officers for cultural events
- **Commercial staff** — trade and investment officers
- **Consular staff** — visa and passport officers
- **Defense staff** — defense attachés and their team
- **Administrative staff** — HR, finance, logistics

**Target the local staff.** They're Indian, they understand your context, they stay longer than diplomats (who rotate every 2-3 years), and they're gatekeepers.

### How to Find and Connect with Local Staff

**1. LinkedIn**
Search "[Embassy Name] New Delhi" and filter by people. You'll find local staff with titles like "Program Manager," "Project Officer," "Senior Officer."

**2. Connecting:**

"Hi [Name], I see you work at the [Embassy Name] in the [Section] section. I'm working on [your project] which overlaps with your work on [specific area]. Would you be open to a quick chat about the [topic] landscape?"

**3. Attend embassy events and specifically engage with the staff** — they're handling logistics, more approachable than diplomats.

## 2.13 Diplomatic Receptions — Etiquette Guide

### Before the Event

**Dress Code:**
- Formal reception: Dark suit (Indian or Western both fine). Women: Saree or formal dress.
- Semi-formal: Blazer + trousers. Kurta-pyjama with Nehru jacket.
- Never: Jeans, T-shirts, flip-flops, too much perfume/cologne.

**What to Carry:**
- Business cards (essential — keep them in a cardholder)
- Phone (camera for photos, but don't take too many)
- Not knowing what to do with hands? Hold a business card or a glass of water.

At the Event:

**Flow:**
1. Arrive on time (Indian Standard Time doesn't apply here — be 5 min early)
2. Register/sign in
3. Find the host (Ambassador or head of mission) — greet them briefly
4. Circulate — don't stay in one group
5. Eat and drink moderately
6. Leave before it ends (diplomatic skill: leave when you've added maximum value)
8n't drink alcohol if you can't handle it. One glass of water or juice in hand is a legitimate social shield.

**Conversation Starters:**
- "What brings you to India?" / "How long have you been in Delhi?"
- "I really enjoyed [speech/panel/event] tonight."
- "I've been following your country's work on [topic]."
- "What's the most surprising thing about India for you?"

Conversation Enders:
- "It was wonderful meeting you. Let me give you my card."
- "I won't keep you longer — I'm sure there are many people wanting to speak with you."
- "Thank you for your time. I'll follow up on email about [topic]."

**Don't:**
- Talk about visa or job at first meeting
- Corner an ambassador for 20 minutes
- Interrupt conversations
- Post negative comments about the event on social media
- Take selfies with everyone (it looks desperate)
- Loudly discuss politics or religion

Following Up

**Within 24 hours:** LinkedIn connection request with personalized note:
"Dear [Name], it was a pleasure meeting you at [event] at [Embassy Name]. I enjoyed our conversation about [topic]. I look forward to staying connected. Best, [Your Name]"

**Within 1 week:** Send a value-add email. A relevant article. An introduction they requested. A follow-up to a conversation point.

## 2.19 Final Words for the Introvert

Diplomatic networking sounds intimidating. It's not. Here's the truth:

**Diplomats are trained to talk to strangers.** They're professionally obligated to be nice to you. Every event they host, they want people like you to attend.

Your superpower is your AI domain expertise. Most people in diplomatic circles talk about politics, economics, and culture. Almost no one talks about AI agents, LLMs, and automation. You are unique. You are needed.

The key is consistency. Not one fancy reception. But showing up to the British Council every month. Reading think tank papers. Sending one LinkedIn note per week.

Start this week. Find one embassy event. Attend. Talk to one person. Follow up.

That's all it takes.
