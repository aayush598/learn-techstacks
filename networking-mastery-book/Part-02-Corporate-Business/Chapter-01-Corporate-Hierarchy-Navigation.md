# Chapter 1: Corporate Hierarchy Navigation

## Understanding Corporate Structures in India

Before you can navigate corporate India, you must understand its structure. Indian companies, especially large ones like a tech company, follow a strict hierarchy. Your ability to navigate this hierarchy determines your career trajectory.

### The Indian Corporate Hierarchy

**Entry Level (0-3 years):**
- Graduate Trainee
- Assistant System Engineer (ASE)
- Software Engineer / Developer
- Junior Analyst

**Junior Management (3-6 years):**
- System Engineer (SE)
- Senior Software Engineer
- Team Lead
- Module Lead

**Middle Management (6-12 years):**
- Project Manager (PM)
- Senior Project Manager
- Delivery Manager
- Program Manager

**Senior Management (12-18 years):**
- Associate Director
- Director
- Senior Director

**Executive Level (18+ years):**
- Vice President (VP)
- Senior VP (SVP)
- Executive VP (EVP)
- CTO / CIO / CXO
- CEO

### The Unspoken Rules of Hierarchy

**Rule 1: Titles matter.** In Indian corporate culture, titles are status markers. When addressing someone, always use their highest title. "Hello, sir" or "Hello, [Designation]" is safer than first names unless invited.

**Rule 2: Hierarchy determines communication.** Information flows top-down. Decisions flow top-down. You don't approach a VP directly unless you have a relationship. You go through your manager → their manager → the VP.

**Rule 3: Proximity to power is everything.** Your immediate manager determines your raises, projects, and promotions. Skip-level managers determine your long-term trajectory. The people at the top determine the culture and opportunities.

**Rule 4: Visibility trumps hard work.** In a company of 500,000 people (a leading IT company has over 600,000), working hard is not enough. Your work must be SEEN by the right people.

### Power Centers in Large Indian Corporations

**1. The Delivery Team:** This is where the actual work happens. Delivery Managers and Project Managers control project allocation. They decide who works on what.

**2. The HR Team:** They control hiring, promotions, and performance reviews. HR Business Partners (HRBPs) have significant influence over your career.

**3. The Sales/Presales Team:** They bring in projects. Being visible to the sales team can get you onto new, high-profile projects.

**4. The Competency/COE Teams:** Centers of Excellence (AI/ML COE, Automation COE) are where specialty skills are showcased. If you join an AI COE, you're visible to leadership.

**5. The Senior Leadership:** VPs and above set the direction. Getting visibility with them requires strategic effort.

## Building Relationships with Seniors and Managers

### The Manager Relationship

Your manager controls your immediate career. Here's how to manage up.

**The Weekly 1:1:**
If your manager doesn't schedule weekly 1:1s, request them. Use this format:
- What I accomplished this week (3 bullet points)
- What I'm stuck on (1-2 blockers)
- What I want to learn (1 skill/area)
- How can I help you? (Always offer value upward)

**Making Your Manager Look Good:**
- Deliver before deadlines
- Flag issues early (never surprise them)
- Suggest improvements that make the team look good
- Speak positively about your manager to others (it gets back to them)
- Take ownership of problems when they arise

**Getting Stretch Assignments:**
"Sir/Ma'am, I've completed my assigned work ahead of schedule. I noticed that [adjacent problem] is something the team is struggling with. I'd like to take a crack at it — would that be okay?"

This shows initiative and signals you want more responsibility.

### Relationships with Skip-Level Managers

Your manager's manager is where real influence lies. But you can't go around your manager.

**The Indirect Approach:**
- Be visible in meetings your skip-level attends
- Speak up with thoughtful contributions
- Ask your skip-level a question in an all-hands meeting
- If they send a company-wide email, reply with a thoughtful response
- Wait for natural opportunities to interact

**The Formal Skip-Level Meeting:**
Some companies have skip-level check-ins. If offered:
- Come prepared with 3 things you've accomplished
- Share 1 improvement idea for the team
- Ask 1 question about their vision
- Don't complain about your manager

## Corporate Events and Town Halls

### Before the Event

**Preparation:**
- Learn who will speak (CEO, CTO, VPs, etc.)
- Read their recent talks/interviews
- Prepare 1-2 thoughtful questions
- Identify 3 people you want to connect with
- Practice your introduction

### During the Event

**The Town Hall Strategy:**
- Arrive 10 minutes early (front-center seats)
- Ask a question during Q&A (this is how executives notice you)
- The question formula: "I appreciate [specific point from talk]. What's your perspective on [related challenge]?"

**The Networking Break:**
- Target 3 conversations, not 30
- Find people standing alone (they're easier to approach)
- Don't talk about your daily work — talk about ideas
- Exchange LinkedIn or business cards
- Make notes immediately after each conversation

### After the Event

**The 24-Hour Follow-up:**
Send LinkedIn connect requests to everyone you spoke with. Include a personalized note referencing your conversation.

For the executive you asked a question to: "Thanks for answering my question at the town hall about [topic]. Your perspective on [specific point] gave me a new way to think about it."

## Performance Visibility Strategies

Hard work is table stakes. Visibility is how you get promoted.

### The Visibility Framework

**1. Document Your Wins:**
Keep a "brag document" with:
- Projects delivered (with metrics)
- Problems solved
- Positive feedback from clients/colleagues
- Skills acquired
- Impact numbers (time saved, revenue generated, cost reduced)

Update this weekly. Use it in performance reviews.

**2. Communicate Your Wins Artfully:**
Don't boast. Instead:
- In team meetings: "I've been experimenting with [approach] and found it reduces processing time by 30%. Happy to share if anyone's interested."
- In status reports: "Completed [project] ahead of schedule, which freed up capacity for [additional work]."
- With your manager: "I'm working on [project]. It's going well — I think it could set a pattern for how we handle similar projects going forward."

**3. Create Visible Artifacts:**
- Documentation that helps the team
- Automation scripts shared internally
- Presentations at internal forums
- Knowledge base articles
- Internal demos of your work

**4. Get External Visibility:**
- Publish on LinkedIn (tag a leading IT company in relevant posts)
- Speak at external events (mention a leading IT company in speaking bio)
- Get certifications (display on LinkedIn with a tech company affiliation)
- Write internal blog posts or newsletter articles

### The Performance Review Strategy

Performance reviews at a tech company typically happen every 6-12 months. Prepare systematically.

**3 months before reviews:**
- Start collecting data on your achievements
- Get a "high performer" reputation by going above and beyond
- Ask for stretch assignments that will generate review-worthy results

**1 month before:**
- Write your self-assessment (use your brag document)
- Ask your manager for specific feedback areas
- Prepare evidence for each claim

**During the review:**
- Focus on impact, not effort
- Use the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result)
- Share your development goals (AI specialization)
- Ask for specific guidance on reaching the next level

## Building a Reputation Within the Organization

Your reputation at a tech company determines your mobility and growth.

### The Reputation Quadrant

**Internal Reputation = Competence + Reliability + Likability + Visibility**

**Competence:** You deliver quality work. You solve problems. People trust your judgment.
**Reliability:** You meet deadlines. You don't overpromise. People count on you.
**Likability:** You're pleasant to work with. You help others. People want you on their team.
**Visibility:** People know who you are and what you've done.

### Building Each Quadrant

**Competence:**
- Deepen your AI skills continuously
- Become the go-to person for AI questions in your team
- Share knowledge proactively

**Reliability:**
- Under-promise and over-deliver
- Communicate proactively about progress and blockers
- Never miss a deadline without advance notice

**Likability:**
- Say thank you genuinely and often
- Help others without being asked
- Be positive in meetings (don't complain)
- Remember names, birthdays, and personal details

**Visibility:**
- Speak in meetings (even if it's one comment)
- Present at internal events
- Share wins with your team AND skip-level managers
- Build relationships outside your immediate team

### The 360-Degree Reputation

Your reputation with different groups matters differently:

**Peers:** They recommend you for projects and promotions. Build peer alliances.
**Juniors:** They amplify or damage your reputation. Be helpful to juniors.
**Managers:** They decide your ratings and raises. Make their lives easier.
**Clients/Stakeholders:** Their feedback carries weight. Exceed their expectations.
