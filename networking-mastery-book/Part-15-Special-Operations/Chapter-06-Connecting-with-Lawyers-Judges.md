# Chapter 6: Connecting with Lawyers and Judges

## Navigating the Corridors of Justice

The legal profession in India is one of the most well-connected, hierarchical, and relationship-driven communities in existence. Lawyers and judges operate on networks built over decades. For a tech professional, entering this world seems impossible — but you have a secret weapon: legal technology (LegalTech) is revolutionizing Indian courts, and the people who need it most are exactly the people you want to meet.

This chapter is your complete guide to building relationships with Supreme Court advocates, High Court judges, law firm partners, corporate general counsels, and the entire Indian legal ecosystem. You will learn how your AI skills open doors that remain closed to most, how to navigate the protocols of legal networking, and how to build lasting relationships in India's most prestigious professional community.

## 3. Connecting with Supreme Court Advocates

The Supreme Court bar is the most exclusive legal club in India. Here's how to enter it.

### Key People to Know in the Supreme Court

| Person/Role | Why Important | How to Approach |
|-------------|---------------|----------------|
| **Senior Advocate (ex-"Law Officer")** | Government-connected, elite | Through chambers juniors, courtroom observation |
| **Senior Advocate (private practice)** | Top litigators (Fali Nariman, Harish Salve, Mukul Rohatgi, etc.) | Very tough. Use public lectures, book launches. |
| **Advocate-on-Record (AOR)** | Filing specialists | More accessible. Need tech help with court filings. |
| **Supreme Court Bar Association (SCBA) Office Bearers** | Control access to SCBA events | Join SCBA library, attend AGM |
| **Supreme Court Staff** | Registry, court masters | Polite interaction, respect their role |
| **Law Clerks to SC Judges** | Direct access to judges | Apply for law clerk program (if law background) or know them socially |

### The Courtroom Observation Strategy

This is your most powerful tool. Anyone can walk into the Supreme Court.

**Step-by-Step Process:**

1. **Plan your visit:** Supreme Court operates Monday-Friday, 10:30 AM to 4:00 PM. Best days are Tuesday-Thursday (fewer holidays).

2. **Entry:** Go to Gate 1 (Tilak Marg). Submit ID at reception. Get visitor pass. No previous appointment needed.

3. **Which court to watch:** Court 1 (CJI's court) hears the most important cases. Arrive before 10:30 AM to get a seat. Other courts are more accessible.

4. **Who to watch:** Identify senior advocates arguing cases. Note their names. Listen carefully. After the hearing, you can approach them (with strict protocol).

5. **The approach:** Write a brief note: *"Respected Sir/Ma'am, I was inspired by your arguments today on [case name]. I'm building [AI legal tool] and would be honored to have your guidance. My number: [XXX]. I will not disturb your work."* Hand it to their junior clerk. Do NOT approach the advocate directly during court hours.

6. **Follow-up:** If they call (unlikely for senior seniors), great. If not, try connecting on LinkedIn after 2-3 court visits where you've seen them argue.

### Supreme Court Bar Association (SCBA) Events

The SCBA regularly hosts:
- **Farewell functions for retiring judges** — open to all advocates
- **Law Day celebrations** (November 26) — major event
- **Constitution Day events** (January 26)
- **Book launches and seminars** — best for networking
- **SCBA library** — you can get temporary membership if introduced by a member

**Strategy:** Attend SCBA public events. You don't need to be a lawyer. Stand near the registration desk. Offer to help with tech (live streaming, QR code registration, etc.).

## 4. High Court and District Court Networking

State High Courts and District Courts are more accessible than the Supreme Court and offer richer networking opportunities because the bar is smaller and more community-oriented.

### Top High Courts to Target

| High Court | Key Features | Networking Strategy |
|------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Delhi High Court** | Most prestigious HC, commercial focus | DHC Bar Association events, Moots |
| **Bombay High Court** | Oldest, corporate-heavy | OVC (Original Side) networking |
| **Madras High Court** | Historic, strong bar | Madras Bar Association events |
| **Calcutta High Court** | Oldest, IP-heavy | Calcutta Bar Association library |
| **Karnataka High Court** | Tech corridor connection | Bangalore Legal Festival |
| **Telangana High Court** | Growing, business-friendly | New bar associations forming |
| **Punjab & Haryana HC** | Chandigarh, strong bar | Punjab Bar Council events |
| **Gujarat High Court** | Business-friendly, growing | Gujarat Legal Forum |

### The High Court Bar Association Strategy

1. **Get a bar association membership** (if you're a law student or legal professional). Some HCs allow "library memberships" for non-lawyers.

2. **Attend bar association meetings** — they're usually monthly and open to members. Be respectful, observe, and slowly introduce yourself.

3. **Volunteer for bar association tech upgrades** — most HC bar associations have outdated websites and systems. Offer to help.

4. **The "Tiffin Strategy":** In many High Courts (especially Delhi, Bombay, Madras), lawyers eat together in bar association canteens. This is the most informal networking setting. Sit at a table with 3-4 junior advocates. Introduce yourself as a tech enthusiast interested in legal tech.

5. **Attend HC farewell functions** for retiring judges. These are always open. Be respectful, dress formally.

## 7. Connecting with Judges (Proper Protocol)

This is the most delicate networking area. Judges in India maintain strict ethical boundaries. You CANNOT build personal relationships with judges easily, but you can build professional connections.

### The Right Way to Connect with Judges

**DO:**
- Address them as "Your Lordship" / "My Lord" / "Your Honour" in court
- Refer to them as "Hon'ble Mr. Justice [Name]" in writing
- If introduced formally, say "Sir/Ma'am" respectfully
- Connect through proper professional channels
- Attend judicial seminars and conferences
- Send respectful congratulations on notable judgments

**DON'T:**
- Approach a judge directly at social events
- Send LinkedIn requests to sitting judges (many judges don't use LinkedIn)
- Ask for personal favors or case-related help
- Discuss pending cases
- Call a judge at home
- Offer gifts or hospitality
- Follow judges on social media (If they have public accounts, engage professionally with their content)

### Appropriate Channels to Connect with Judges

**1. Judicial Academy Programs**

Every state has a Judicial Academy that trains judges. Many have public lectures and seminars.

| Judicial Academy | Location | Program Type |
|-----------------|----------|--------------|
| **National Judicial Academy** | Bhopal | National-level programs (elite) |
| **Delhi Judicial Academy** | Delhi | State-level training |
| **Maharashtra Judicial Academy** | Mumbai | State-level, accessible |
| **Gujarat Judicial Academy** | Gandhinagar | State-level, modern approach |
| **Karnataka Judicial Academy** | Bangalore | State-level, tech-friendly |
| **UP Judicial Training & Research Institute** | Lucknow | Large state network |

**Strategy:** Find public lectures and seminars at these academies. Many are free. Register, attend, and ask thoughtful questions.

**2. Law Commission of India**

The Law Commission (21st Law Commission currently) works on legal reforms. They invite public comments on consultation papers. Submitting quality comments gets you noticed.

**3. Court Allotment Ceremonies**

At the start of each year, High Courts have ceremonies where new judges are sworn in. These are public events. Attend, observe, and if you know someone being sworn in, a simple congratulations is appropriate.

**4. Judgment Release Events**

When a landmark judgment is pronounced, lawyers sometimes organize seminars to discuss it. These are open and judges sometimes attend as guests.

**5. International Judicial Conferences**

Organizations like the International Association of Judges, Commonwealth Magistrates' and Judges' Association host events that Indian judges attend.

## 15. Final Words for the Introvert Entering Legal Networks

The legal profession in India is hierarchical, traditional, and intimidating. But it is also a community that respects intelligence, hard work, and genuine interest. Starting with limited connections, you have advantages:

1. **You listen more than you talk** — lawyers love people who listen
2. **You think before you speak** — your words carry weight
3. **You bring tech skills** — valued more in law than in most professions
4. **You have no agenda** — you're not (yet) competing with anyone in law

**Your entry point is always legal tech. Your sustainer is genuine interest. Your escalator is consistent value delivery.**

Start with one Supreme Court visit. Sit in Court No. 1. Listen to arguments for an hour. You'll realize that judges and lawyers are just people doing complex work. And like everyone else, they appreciate someone who makes their work easier.

The Chief Justice of India, the Attorney General, the most senior partners at Cyril Amarchand — all of them started somewhere. All of them need technology partners. All of them are approachable if you have the right opening.

Your legal networking journey begins not with a LinkedIn request, but with a single step into a courtroom. Take that step tomorrow.
